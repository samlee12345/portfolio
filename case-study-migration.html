Overview

A hard deadline, known disruptions, and a plan to ship anyway.

Fidessa had a business mandate to force-migrate users from its legacy Windows platform to its newer web-based platform within a 9-month deadline — driven by Microsoft's deprecation of VB6, the technology underlying the legacy system. My role was to investigate UX concerns on the new platform and design an onboarding flow to prepare users for the transition.

The original plan included usability testing to confirm whether users would be negatively impacted by the known concerns. I never ran that test — because I was able to call out the consequences before it.

Through deep knowledge of how these user personas think and behave, I identified that concerns the team considered minor were likely to cause serious friction. I made the case for urgency. The dev team reprioritized, and 5 of 7 critical blockers were resolved before launch.

The Problem

The migration plan underestimated who would be most disrupted.

Stated ask

Capture usability concerns introduced by running the legacy and new platforms side-by-side, and design solutions to address them before the February 2026 release.

Actual problem

The migration plan underestimated how disruptive the transition would be — especially for US trader segment users, who had been left out of the original scope entirely. Unlike European and Asian users, whose desktops would be fully native to the new platform, US segment users would work in a mixed environment: a combination of legacy and new platform tools running simultaneously, because the new platform had only ~5% feature parity with the legacy version for their regional workflows. The original plan was to ship anyway, with known concerns unresolved.

Why it matters: Fidessa serves 600+ global financial firms and 3 million users. A migration that disrupts live trading workflows is not a UX inconvenience — it is a business and revenue risk. The PM's own framing: conduct an impact assessment with clients to avoid damaging revenue.

Understanding the Domain

Seven concerns. Two platforms. One trader who won't tolerate disruption.

These aren't users who adapt quickly to change. A color shift, a missing tab, a right-click menu that behaves differently — any of these would generate complaints and erode trust in the platform.

I reviewed actual trader desktop setups across user segments and documented each usability concern systematically: context, user impact, design options, and whether it was solvable by design or required dev changes.

01

Visual differences

Traders use desktop theming as a workflow cue. Running both platforms simultaneously creates a mixed-theme desktop with no consistent visual language.

02

Mixed desktop layouts

Traders organize their desktops using tabs and grouped windows. When a layout includes tools from both platforms, migration automatically breaks it apart. Screen real estate is critical for traders.

03

Triggered alerts

Different alert types surface in different locations depending on which platform the source tool belongs to.

04

Alert configuration

Setting up and managing alerts requires navigating two separate systems, one per platform.

05

Loss of right-click menu options

Some grids in the new platform are missing right-click options that exist in their legacy equivalents, affecting region-specific workflows.

06

Data transfer between interoped tools

Data does not always carry across cleanly when an action in one platform triggers a tool in the other.

07

Accessing legacy-only tools

Tools that exist only in the legacy platform must be accessed via the legacy toolbar, which sits in an unexpected location in the new environment.

Additionally: US segment users had been excluded from the original scope entirely, despite facing a harder migration experience due to lower feature parity in their regional workflows.

Defining the Scope

Ship with known disruptions, or fix them first.

The original plan was to ship with known concerns and address them post-launch. My collaborator and I pushed back: the concerns were more disruptive than the plan assumed, and there was no realistic dev bandwidth to fix them after launch either.

Option 1 — Ship as planned

Ship with the February 2026 deadline. Conduct usability testing to document the disruption and use results to make the case to stakeholders post-launch.

Option 2 — Rescope

Address the highest-impact blockers before forcing users to migrate.

Result

7 critical blockers → 5 resolved before the February 2026 launch. The 2 remaining concerns could not be resolved within the timeline.

I didn't wait for usability testing to make the case. I had enough understanding of these users — their low tolerance for change, their reliance on established desktop layouts, the way they use visual cues as workflow signals — to call out the consequences directly. The PM agreed, and development was reprioritized.

Design Process

Two workstreams: design where it could help, documentation where it couldn't.

Most of this project was not UI design. It was investigation, documentation, and advocacy — identifying what was going to break and making the case for fixing it before release.

Decision 1

Onboarding flow

Users opening the new platform for the first time needed to know what had changed before they encountered it. The announcement covered: the mixed tool environment and why it exists, new alert functionality and location, desktop navigation changes, and notice that previously grouped or tabbed tools may no longer be contained. Theming was acknowledged transparently — original theming options committed for a future release.

Decision 2

Alert unification

Proposed combining all alert configuration under a single location to reduce fragmentation between the two platforms' alert systems.

Decision 3

Impact documentation

Concerns like layout decoupling and the US segment dual-version problem had no design solution within dev constraints. The work was documenting the impact clearly enough that the PM could act on it.

Validation

No formal testing. The concerns were resolved before it was needed.

The original plan included usability testing to gather data on user reactions. As concerns were resolved one by one, the need shifted. By launch, the two remaining concerns either could not be addressed by design (theming) or were statistically unlikely to affect the vast majority of users — layout decoupling affected less than 1% of typical desktop setups based on tool usage data.

Making the case without test data was the harder path. It required specificity: not "users might be confused" but "this persona relies on grouped window layouts as a workflow signal, and breaking that will generate complaints from day one."

Reflection

Advocacy is design work.

This project had no UI design deliverables for most of its duration. The work was investigation, documentation, and advocacy — figuring out what was going to break and making the case for fixing it before release.

The hardest part was holding the position that these concerns were serious enough to address first, against a hard deadline and team momentum to ship. Doing it with enough specificity that the PM could act required sustained effort over several months.

What I'd do differently: quantitative usability data gathered earlier would have made the advocacy faster. Getting that data before launch pressure set in would have shortened the time between identifying a concern and getting it prioritized.
