Overview

"The system wasn't broken by bugs; it was broken by fragmentation. Users didn't need new alerts—they needed a way to manage the ones they already had."

Fidessa's alerting system was a legacy labyrinth. Originally designed for simple price triggers, it had ballooned into a disparate collection of notification modules that failed to serve the modern institutional trader. The mandate was simple: "Make alerts better." But for a senior practitioner, the task was to unearth why they were failing in the first place.

Project Timeline

Discovery Audit Concept Design Ship

The Problem

"The brief was too narrow. The real problem was structural."

Our initial research revealed a staggering statistic: only 13% of active traders were using the alerting suite. In a high-velocity environment where market signals are everything, this was a massive failure.

13% Active Usage
87% Opportunity Gap

Quantitative data showed that while alerts were being triggered, they were rarely acted upon. Users described the experience as "shouting into the void"—too much noise, no clear priority, and no unified interface.

Understanding the Domain

"66% of users wanted alerts but couldn't navigate the system."

Deep-dive discovery revealed a paradox. 66% of surveyed users expressed a high desire for complex alerting capabilities, yet these same users couldn't navigate the existing menu structure.

Defining the Scope

"Additive improvements don't fix fragmentation."

The original brief called for "additive improvements"—new alert types. I pushed back. Adding more alert types to a broken delivery system was akin to buying more luggage for a plane that couldn't take off. I redefined the scope to focus on a centralized Alert Hub.

The Ask

"Add 5 new price-action triggers."

The Pivot

"Create a unified command center for all signals."

Design Process

"One hub, three personas, no separate flows."

The solution required balancing three distinct personas: the Day Trader (speed), the Risk Manager (compliance), and the Portfolio Manager (long-term strategy). Instead of building separate flows, I designed a modular Hub where the UI adapted based on the user's focus.

Outcome

"First version shipped. Full redesign in backlog."

The first version of the Hub was shipped to key accounts in Q4. While the full visual redesign remains in the product backlog, the structural changes—the "Centralized Hub" logic—have become the new standard for all Fidessa platform upgrades moving forward.

Reflection

"Push back before designs exist, not after."

This project reinforced the most critical lesson of senior design: **The best design is often a structural argument.** If I had simply followed the initial brief, we would have delivered "better" versions of tools that nobody used.

By pushing back early and redefining the problem as one of architecture rather than features, I was able to deliver value that scaled across the entire ecosystem.
