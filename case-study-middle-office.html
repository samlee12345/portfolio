Part One

Project Overview

The Mission

Fidessa is an enterprise platform for middle office operations, managing the post-trade settlement lifecycle across global financial firms. European firms are moving to trade plus one day (T+1), a regulatory requirement to settle trades within one business day, and need to understand where their delays are concentrated.

Key Responsibilities

  • End-to-end ownership across domain research, user research, design, and validation
  • Knowledge transfer sessions with product managers to build allocations workflow fluency
  • Discovery workshops to map the end-to-end workflow and define the scope
  • Design of the dashboard, analytics views, and firm and client level summaries

The Problem

Operations teams had no historical view of their allocations. Root causes fall into three categories: client data, order data, or service errors. Without historical data, there was no way to identify which type was responsible or whether a problem was a one-off or a recurring pattern.

Stated ask

Speed up the allocation process.

User Sentiment

  • No visibility into patterns: Teams resolved the same delays week after week with no way to tell whether a problem was a one-off or a systemic pattern. Without that distinction, every incident started from scratch and the same root causes kept recurring.
  • No data to push for change: When delays traced back to client data quality, teams had no documented evidence to put in front of clients. Without data, there was no basis to push clients to correct how they sent information, and the same problems repeated.
  • T+1 exposure: American firms already face financial penalties for missed T+1. European firms face the same regulatory requirement soon, with no current way to identify or address the sources of their delays.

Business Impact

  • Negative impression: Middle office teams are high-visibility. A platform without analytical tooling to support a regulatory requirement surfaces as a failure in renewal conversations.
  • Competitive gap: Competitor platforms are moving toward analytics as standard. Every quarter without this feature, accounts have a stronger reason to evaluate alternatives.
  • Regulatory alignment gap: T+1 is a regulatory shift client firms must meet. A platform that doesn't help them get there becomes a liability rather than an asset, and shows up in churn.

The Solution

A historical analytics layer giving operations teams visibility into field mismatches, client response delays, and service mismatches across firm and client views, with trend analysis and built-in export.

Field mismatches

Most frequently incorrect fields, by severity and resolution time.

Client response delay

Clients consistently slow to act on correction requests, surfaced over time.

Service mismatches

Confirmation mismatches by service (CTM, AMS, L1/L2), isolated from data quality issues.

My Impact

Addressing User Needs

  • Every analytics category maps directly to a root cause type surfaced in research: field mismatches, client response delays, or service errors.
  • The dashboard opens in a default state that immediately surfaces priority clients, amended fields, and service errors. No configuration required.
  • Field-level data is exportable and printable, designed to travel beyond the platform into client review conversations.

Organizational Influence

  • The dashboard layout and component approach established a pattern that can be extended to other analytical views across the platform.
  • The first design-led process for middle office at Fidessa. No prior UX work existed for this part of the platform.

User Research

12 middle office professionals interviewed across European and American firms, including one on-site session at a client trading office. Responsibilities vary significantly by firm size. At smaller firms, middle office staff carry both operational and trading duties, which shaped how allocation delays affected their day.

Each session focused on three questions: what breaks down, where the time goes, and what data would change how they work.

Research Findings

The majority of allocations are processed at market close, giving traders a narrow window to resolve exceptions. When resolution depends on a client responding, that window shrinks further. Under T+1, there is no buffer. Unresolved interventions mean missed deadlines and direct financial penalties. Reducing data mismatches and manual intervention is not a workflow improvement. It is a compliance requirement.

83% Had no systematic way to identify what was causing their allocation delays
58% Believed most manual interventions traced back to incorrect static data passed by clients
66% Would use historical analytics to prepare for and conduct client review conversations

Key Insights

Operations teams knew delays were happening. Without historical data, they had no way to know why, which clients were responsible, or whether anything was improving over time.

Three root types

Root causes always fall into one of three categories: client data, order data, or service errors. Structuring analytics by type makes the data immediately actionable.

Trends over snapshots

Point-in-time visibility is not enough. Historical trends show which clients and fields are consistently the source of delays, shifting the insight from reactive to anticipatory.

Data travels to clients

Analytics travel beyond the platform into client conversations. Export and print need to be designed in from the start, not added later.

Grid-based workflows are deeply ingrained

Operations staff configure, filter, and navigate data grids throughout their day. The right approach adds a new analytical layer on top of existing workflows, not a replacement for them.

Design Decisions

Decision 1

Configurable Grid vs Preset Dashboard

Middle office professionals configure, filter, and navigate data grids throughout their day. The grid model fits naturally into existing workflows. However, the root causes of allocation delays always fall into one of three fixed categories: field mismatches, client response delays, or service mismatches. A configurable grid would require users to set up their own groupings before seeing anything useful. The preset dashboard opens directly on the three root cause categories, with no configuration required.

Configurable grid explored

Preset dashboard chosen

Grid (left) surfaces the right data but requires upfront grouping setup. Preset dashboard (right) lands immediately on the three root cause categories without any configuration.

Decision 2

Export and Print as a First-Class Feature

Export and print came up unprompted across multiple research sessions. Operations teams hold regular client review meetings, and the analytics needed to be useful beyond the platform, in the conversations where accountability decisions are made. Export was designed in from the start rather than added later.

01Firm level and client level views

Two views serve two distinct operational contexts. Firm level shows aggregated data across all clients. Client level drills into a specific client, showing the same data filtered to that relationship. The toggle at the top keeps context clear and the transition fast.

Firm level

For understanding overall T+1 performance and identifying which clients to prioritize. The default landing view.

Client level

For client conversations. Drill-down to a specific client gives the team data they can walk through in a review meeting.

02Time-based trend at the top

A trend line showing the percentage of orders not meeting T+1 over time anchors the dashboard before users drill into category-level data. A 23% non-compliance rate means something different if the prior month was 31% than if it was 14%. Without the trend, teams are always reading the current number without context.

03Three analytics categories

Each category maps to one of the three root cause types identified in research. Separating them by type makes the data immediately actionable. A team investigating a client complaint goes to Client Analytics; a team investigating a recurring field error goes to Field Analytics.

Overview

No scope, no problem statement, no existing design.

Fidessa's middle office operations teams, approximately 300,000 professionals across 600+ global financial firms, manage the post-trade settlement lifecycle. I was asked to speed up the allocation process ahead of European T+1 regulatory requirements. There was no defined scope, no clear problem statement, and no existing design to build from.

I led the project end-to-end as the sole designer: from domain research and problem definition through discovery workshops, design, and one round of validation with operations staff.

The Problem

No brief. No data. No way to be proactive.

Stated ask

Speed up the allocation process.

Actual problem

Operations teams had no historical or analytical view of their allocations. When delays occurred, there was no data to help staff identify patterns. Which clients were causing delays, how often, or why. Without that visibility, teams couldn't be proactive: they couldn't address root causes, and they couldn't tell clients to correct how they were sending information. Everything was reactive.

Why it matters: a regulatory change is driving urgency. European firms are moving to T+1 allocation completion, a requirement the Americas already meet. Teams that cannot identify and address sources of delays before that transition will be directly exposed to compliance risk.

Understanding the Domain

The allocations workflow is more complex than it looks.

Before making any design decisions, I needed to understand how the allocations workflow actually worked: the data, the services, the tools, and where breakdowns occurred.

I conducted knowledge transfer sessions with PMs to build domain fluency, then led cross-functional discovery workshops to map the end-to-end workflow. Output: workflow maps and information maps covering all services and functionalities users currently interact with.

What surfaced: allocation delays consistently trace back to one of three root cause types: client data, order data, or service errors. Operations teams had no way to surface which type was responsible in a given case, let alone see patterns over time. The entire workflow was reactive and queue-based, with no historical layer at all.

End-to-end allocations workflow map produced during knowledge transfer sessions with PMs.

Defining the Scope

Three problem areas. One design vehicle.

The original ask gave nothing to constrain against. Through discovery and user research, three distinct problem areas emerged.

01

Dashboard

Fragmented views of the same workflow displayed across separate grids with no consolidated analytical layer

02

Summary analytics

No data on which clients, fields, or services generate the most delays or require the most manual intervention

03

Export and print

Operations teams hold regular client reviews and need a way to bring analytics data into those conversations

Middle office operations staff are expert users with deeply ingrained workflows. Any changes to how they currently work would create significant disruption. The dashboard approach added a new analytical layer without forcing changes to existing workflows, surfacing the information they needed without asking them to work differently.

Design Process

Tables, not charts. Default state, not configuration.

The core design challenge was visualization: how do you represent the health of thousands of client accounts and allocation batches in a way that lets an operations professional immediately see where to focus?

Operations staff are highly comfortable with data tables. The goal was not to replace tables with charts, but to design tables where the default state surfaces a clear next step. Opening the dashboard, a user should immediately see which clients are creating the most noise and know what to raise in a client conversation, without configuring anything first.

Decision 1

Preset grids structured around the three root cause categories

Root causes always fall into one of three types: client data, order data, or service errors. Preset grids for each category let users arrive at the relevant data without any setup. Firm level and client level views serve two distinct operational contexts: one for internal prioritization, one for client conversations.

Decision 2

Surfacing data at the client and field level over time

To enable proactive management, the dashboard presents aggregate data historically. Time filters (6 months to 3 years) let teams see trends: which clients are repeatedly causing delays, which fields are consistently amended, and whether the overall situation is improving or worsening.

Decision 3

Export and print as a first-class feature

Research surfaced export and print unprompted across multiple sessions. Teams hold regular client reviews. Designing for export from the start ensures the analytics are useful beyond the platform, in the conversations where accountability decisions are made.

Delivered design

Manual intervention summary: firm level and client level views showing T+1 trend, client analytics, field analytics, and service analytics.

Reflection

Domain first. Design second.

This project required significant domain learning before any design work could start. The allocations workflow, the relationships between services, and how delays are attributed at the client and field level were not things I could assume going in. They had to be learned from the ground up through knowledge transfer and workshop facilitation.

What I'd do differently: engage users earlier. Setting up interviews took longer than it should have. Earlier outreach would have compressed the discovery phase and brought real user perspectives into scope decisions sooner.
