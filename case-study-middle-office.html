Part One

Project Overview

The Mission

Fidessa is an enterprise platform used by middle office operations teams across 600+ global financial firms. These teams manage the post-trade settlement lifecycle: trade confirmation, allocation matching, and exception handling. Across all firms, approximately 300,000 professionals use the platform daily.

A regulatory change is driving urgency. European firms are moving to T+1 allocation completion, a requirement the Americas already meet. To prepare, firms need to understand where their allocation delays are coming from. The brief was to speed up the allocation process. No scope was defined, no problem statement existed, and no prior design work had been done. I built all of it from scratch.

Key Responsibilities

  • End-to-end ownership across domain research, user research, design, and validation
  • Knowledge transfer sessions with PMs to build allocations workflow fluency
  • Discovery workshops to map the end-to-end workflow and define the scope
  • User research across 12 middle office traders in Europe and the Americas
  • Design of the dashboard, analytics views, and firm and client level summaries

The Problem

Operations teams had no historical view of their allocations. When delays occurred, there was no way to identify root causes systematically. Root causes always fall into one of three categories: client data (incorrect information passed by the client), order data (field-level issues), or service errors (mismatches across confirmation services).

Without historical data, teams could not tell whether a problem was a one-off incident or a systemic pattern. When the root cause was on the client side, there was no evidence to bring to the client. Everything was reactive. Teams could not prepare for T+1 requirements without first knowing where delays were concentrated.

Stated ask

Speed up the allocation process.

Actual problem

  • No pattern recognition: without historical data, teams cannot distinguish a systemic problem from a one-off incident
  • No accountability mechanism: when delays are caused by incorrect client data, teams have no evidence to support the conversation
  • Fully reactive: teams respond to delays as they happen rather than addressing the root causes that produce them
  • T+1 exposure: in the Americas, T+1 is already a regulatory requirement and firms face financial penalties when allocations are not completed on time. American clients requested this feature directly because of those penalties. European firms face the same requirement soon, and will be subject to the same fines once the rule is in place. Neither region can prepare without first understanding where their delays are concentrated.

The Solution

A dashboard surfacing the three types of allocation delay: static field mismatches, client response cycles, and service mismatches. Firm-level and client-level views support two distinct operational contexts. Time-based trend analysis (6 months to 3 years) shows whether the situation is improving, not just what is failing today. Export and print built in from the start, so teams can bring data directly into client conversations rather than compiling it manually.

Field mismatches

Which static data fields are most frequently incorrect, how severe each amendment is, and how long it takes to resolve. Identifies the specific data quality issues driving the most delays so teams and clients can address them systematically.

Client response delay

A mismatch has been identified, but the allocation is not meeting T+1 because the client is taking longer than average to respond. Surfaces which clients are consistently slow to act on correction requests, so teams can address the communication cycle, not just the individual incident.

Service mismatches

Trade confirmation mismatches broken down by service type (CTM, AMS, L1/L2 data). Separates service-layer errors from data quality issues so each root cause can be addressed independently.

My Impact

The project brief was to speed up the allocation process for middle office traders who were not completing allocations within the T+1 window. In the Americas, T+1 is already a regulatory requirement with financial penalties for non-compliance. American clients requested this feature directly because of those fines. European firms face the same requirement soon. No scope was defined, no prior UX work existed, and the problem had not been formally articulated. I translated a single directive into a structured analytical solution through domain research and user interviews.

Project Contributions

  • Scope definition: translated a vague mandate into three concrete problem areas with a clear design vehicle
  • First historical analytics layer: the platform had no analytical view of allocations before this project
  • Research-driven structure: the three analytics categories map directly to the three root cause types identified through user interviews
  • T+1 framing: established the regulatory deadline as the primary urgency driver, anchoring scope decisions in a concrete business outcome

Part Two

Discover

Domain Research

Before any design work, I needed fluency in the allocations workflow: the data, the tools, the services, and where breakdowns occurred. I ran knowledge transfer sessions with PMs and led cross-functional discovery workshops to map the end-to-end process.

Output: workflow maps and information maps covering all services and functionalities users interact with, documented in FigJam. These sessions also established the scope. Domain research — workflow and tools map

End-to-end allocations workflow mapped across services (CTM, AMS, CAP), tool landscape, and current-state screens. Produced during knowledge transfer sessions with PMs.

What those sessions surfaced

Finding

No historical data layer exists

Teams can only see the current state of their queue. There is no view of past allocations, no way to aggregate by client or field, and no mechanism for identifying what has been causing delays over time.

Finding

Three types of root cause

Allocation delays consistently trace back to one of three sources: incorrect data passed by the client, field-level issues within the order, or mismatches at the service layer (CTM, AMS, L1/L2 data). These three types form the structural backbone of the analytics design.

Finding

Grid-based workflows are deeply ingrained

Operations staff configure, filter, and navigate data grids throughout their day. Any design that disrupts existing grid-based workflows creates friction for expert users. The right approach adds a new analytical layer on top of existing workflows, not a replacement for them.

User Research

12 middle office traders interviewed across European and American firms. One session was conducted on-site at a client trading office; the remaining sessions were remote video calls. Middle office traders at different firms carry different responsibilities: at smaller firms, they also take on operational duties alongside their trading role.

The goal of each session was to understand pain points within the allocations workflow: what breaks down, where the time goes, and what data would change how they work.

No historical visibility

"I can tell you what's failing today. I can't tell you if it's been failing every Monday for six months."

Workflow need

Historical view of allocations to identify whether delays are isolated incidents or recurring patterns.

Current gap

Queue-only visibility. No aggregated data, no time horizon, no way to see patterns across clients or fields.

Client-side accountability

"We know the client is sending bad data, but we have nothing to show them. It's our word against theirs."

Workflow need

Documented evidence of which clients are passing incorrect data, and how often, to support client conversations.

Current gap

No aggregated client-level data. Root cause identification is manual and case-by-case, with no retained history.

Data for client meetings

"I have quarterly reviews with clients. Right now I go in with nothing on paper. I need something I can put in front of them."

Workflow need

Exportable or printable analytics that can be walked through in client review meetings.

Current gap

No analytical output exists. Any data brought to a client meeting must be manually compiled each time.

Research Findings

The core finding was structural: without historical data, there is no way to distinguish a systemic problem from a one-off incident. Teams respond to the same root causes repeatedly because they have no view of the pattern. Most participants said access to analytics would meaningfully change how they work. A minority were more neutral but still saw the value. Export and print came up unprompted in multiple sessions — teams hold regular client reviews and need data they can walk through.

Root cause attribution

When a delay traces back to a client passing incorrect data, operations teams face a specific problem: they know the root cause, but they have no documented evidence to bring to the client. Without data, the conversation stalls. The client may dispute the characterization, and the same problem recurs the following week. Historical field-level analytics changes this. It makes the pattern visible and the conversation concrete.

Key Insights

Three root types

Root causes always fall into one of three categories: client data, order data, or service errors. Surfacing them by category makes the information immediately actionable rather than requiring manual investigation before a pattern becomes visible.

Trends over snapshots

Point-in-time visibility is not enough. Teams need to see which clients and fields are consistently the source of delays, not just what is failing today. Historical trend data changes the nature of the insight from reactive to anticipatory.

Data travels to clients

The value of analytics is not only internal. Teams bring this data into client conversations, and that use case requires export and print to be designed in from the start, not added as an afterthought.

Part Three

Define

Defining the Scope

The original ask gave nothing to constrain against. Through discovery, scope was clarified: any pain point within the allocations workflow is in scope. Three distinct problem areas emerged from the research.

01

Dashboard

Fragmented views of the same workflow spread across separate grids with no consolidated view

02

Summary analytics

No historical data on which clients, fields, or services are causing the most delays or requiring the most manual intervention

03

Export and print

Operations teams hold regular client reviews and need a way to bring analytics data directly into those conversations

Middle office operations staff are expert users of the platform with deeply ingrained workflows. Any design that changes how they currently work creates disruption. The right vehicle was a new analytical layer that sits alongside existing workflows, not one that replaces them. A dashboard adds visibility without asking users to work differently.

Design Decisions

Decision 1

Configurable Grid vs Preset Dashboard

Middle office operations staff configure, filter, and navigate data grids fluently every day — the grid model offers maximum flexibility and fits naturally into their existing workflow. However, root causes of allocation delays always fall into one of three types: field mismatches, client response delays, or service mismatches. That structure is fixed. A configurable grid would require users to set up their own groupings and filters before seeing anything useful, adding work without adding insight. The preset dashboard solved the cold-start problem: opening it immediately surfaces the most important data across all three root cause categories, with no configuration required.

Configurable grid explored

Preset dashboard chosen

Grid (left) surfaces the right data but requires upfront grouping setup. Preset dashboard (right) lands immediately on the three root cause categories without any configuration.

Decision 2

Export and print as a first-class feature

Research surfaced export and print unprompted across multiple sessions. Operations teams hold regular client review meetings. Designing export in from the start ensures the analytics are useful beyond the platform, in the conversations where accountability decisions are made.

Part Four

Develop

The core design challenge was visualization: how do you represent the health of thousands of client accounts and allocation batches in a way that lets an operations professional immediately see where to focus?

Operations staff are highly comfortable with data tables. The goal was not to replace tables with charts, but to design tables where the default state surfaces a clear next step. Opening the dashboard, a user should immediately see which clients are creating the most noise, know what to raise in a client conversation, and be able to export that view without any configuration first.

Design Decisions

01Firm level and client level views

Two views serve two distinct operational contexts. Firm level shows aggregated data across all clients: the overall T+1 performance, which clients require the most manual intervention, and which fields are most frequently amended across the book. Client level drills into a specific client, showing the same data filtered to that relationship. The toggle at the top keeps context clear and the transition fast.

Firm level

For understanding overall T+1 performance and identifying which clients to prioritize. The default landing view.

Client level

For client conversations. Drill-down to a specific client gives the team data they can walk through in a review meeting.

02Three analytics categories

Each category maps to one of the three root cause types identified in research. Separating them by type makes the information immediately actionable: a team looking at a client complaint about manual intervention goes to Client Analytics. A team investigating why a particular field keeps causing delays goes to Field Analytics.

Client Analytics

Clients not meeting T+1, ranked by order volume. Top clients requiring manual intervention. Percentage of total firm orders. Makes client-side accountability visible and quantified.

Field Analytics

Top amended fields by frequency, severity, and average resolution time. High-severity amendments broken out separately. Identifies which data quality problems are most costly to resolve.

Service Analytics

Mismatched trades broken down by mismatch type across services (CTM, AMS, L1/L2 data). Surfaces service-layer errors separately from client or field-level issues.

03Time-based trend at the top

A trend line showing the percentage of orders not meeting T+1 over time (6 months, 1 year, 2 years, 3 years) anchors the dashboard before users drill into category-level data. It answers the question teams most need answered first: is the situation improving or getting worse? Time filters at the top propagate across all three analytics categories below.

Why it matters

The trend view changes the nature of the insight from a snapshot to a trajectory. A 23% non-compliance rate means something different if the prior month was 31% than if it was 14%. Without the trend, teams are always reading the current number without context.

Dashboard view — delivered design

Manual intervention summary: firm level (left) and client level (right). Firm level shows aggregated performance across all clients. Client level filters all three analytics categories to a specific client for use in review conversations.

The dashboard exceeded the KPI target: a 23% reduction in allocations not meeting T+1, against a target of 15%. The result is notable given that Fidessa has no built-in mechanism for notifying users about new features. Discovery happens through firm-level communication and word of mouth, which means adoption builds gradually after release. The performance exceeded expectations even before full adoption across the user base.

Reflection

Domain first. Design second.

This project required significant domain learning before any design work could start. The allocations workflow, the relationships between services, and how delays are attributed at the client and field level were not things I could assume going in. They had to be learned through sustained knowledge transfer sessions before design decisions could feel grounded rather than guessed.

What I'd do differently: engage users earlier. Setting up interviews took longer than it should have. Earlier outreach would have compressed the discovery phase and brought real user perspectives into scope decisions sooner.
