Part One · Project Overview

The Mission

T+1 · post-trade settlement

Fidessa is a platform used across 6,500+ firms. Middle office operations teams (those responsible for post-trade processing, confirming and settling trades after they execute) are a core user group. I joined as the first and only UX hire, with no brief and no existing design to reference.

European firms are moving to T+1 settlement (a regulatory requirement to settle trades within one business day of execution). They need to understand where their allocation delays are concentrated. No analytical layer existed.

03
Part One · Project Overview

The Problem

Stated ask: "speed up allocations."

Operations teams had no historical view of their allocations (the process of confirming which trades belong to which client accounts after execution). Root causes fall into three categories, but without retained data, they couldn't tell a one-off from a pattern.

I uncovered this through discovery workshops and direct user interviews. The problem hadn't been formally articulated before.

User sentiment

  • No visibility into patterns — teams resolved the same delays week after week with no way to tell a one-off from a systemic recurrence.
  • No data to push for change — when delays traced to client data, teams had no documented evidence to put in front of clients. Word against word.
  • T+1 exposure — Americans already face penalties for missed T+1. Europeans face the same requirement soon, with no current way to address sources of delay.

Business impact

  • Renewal risk — middle office teams are high-visibility. A platform without analytical tooling for a regulatory requirement reads as failure in renewal conversations.
  • Competitive gap — competitor platforms are moving toward analytics as standard. Every quarter without it is another reason to evaluate alternatives.
  • Regulatory misalignment — a platform that doesn't help clients hit T+1 becomes a liability, not an asset.

Part Two · Discover

User Research

12 interviews · 1 on-site floor walk

I ran 12 interviews and one on-site floor walk. Three questions per session: what breaks down, where the time goes, and what data would change how they work.

No historical visibility

"I can tell you what's failing today. I can't tell you if it's been failing every Monday for six months."

Workflow need

Historical view of allocations to identify whether delays are isolated incidents or recurring patterns.

Current gap

Queue-only visibility. No aggregated data, no time horizon, no patterns across clients or fields.

Client-side accountability

"We know the client is sending bad data, but we have nothing to show them. It's our word against theirs."

Workflow need

Documented evidence of which clients pass incorrect data, and how often, to support client conversations.

Current gap

No aggregated client-level data. Root cause identification is manual and case-by-case, with no retained history.

Data for client meetings

"I have quarterly reviews with clients. Right now I go in with nothing on paper. I need something I can put in front of them."

Workflow need

Exportable or printable analytics that can be walked through in client review meetings.

Current gap

Research Findings

Compliance, not workflow

Most allocations process at market close — a narrow window to resolve exceptions. Under T+1, there is no buffer. Reducing data mismatches isn't a workflow improvement; it's a compliance requirement.

Operations teams could see what was failing today, but not whether it was the same problem from last week, last month, or last quarter.

58%

Most manual interventions, in their view, traced back to incorrect static data passed by clients — but without retained data, they couldn't prove it.

Quarterly conversations with clients lacked anything concrete. Teams wanted printable analytics they could walk through in meetings.

Part Two · Discover

Key Insights

From research → design intent

I found that operations teams knew delays were happening. Without historical data, they had no way to know why, who was responsible, or whether anything was improving over time.

01

Three root types

Root causes always fall into one of three categories: client data, order data, or service errors. Structuring analytics by type makes the data immediately actionable.

02

Trends over snapshots

Point-in-time visibility isn't enough. Historical trends show which clients and fields are consistently the source — shifting insight from reactive to anticipatory.

03

Data travels to clients

Analytics travel beyond the platform into client conversations. Export and print need to be designed in from the start, not added later.

04

Grids are the native idiom

Operations staff configure, filter, and navigate data grids all day. The right approach adds an analytical layer on top of existing workflows — not a replacement.

Part Three · Define

Defining the Scope

Workflow understood · data missing

No route existed. I had to read the terrain first.

My research and discovery workshops confirmed the allocations workflow was well understood. The gap I identified was systematic: teams had no way to distinguish recurring root causes from one-off exceptions.

Original ask

"Speed up the allocation process."

Clarified scope

A dashboard surfacing historical allocation analytics across firm and client views — the missing analytical layer that let teams identify whether a problem was a pattern or a one-off.

"Speed up allocations" was the full extent of what I was given. No historical data had ever been retained. I had to define the problem before I could design the solution.

09
Part Three · Define · Decision 01

Configurable Grid vs Preset Dashboard

Native idiom vs. zero-config
A

Configurable grid

Operations professionals navigate grids all day. The model fits naturally — but a configurable grid requires users to set up their own groupings before seeing anything useful. High ceiling, high floor.

Right tool for power users. Wrong tool for a first analytical layer where no defaults exist yet.

B

Preset dashboard

Root causes always fall into the same three categories. A preset dashboard opens directly on them — no configuration required, immediate utility on first load.

Chosen. Zero-config landing experience. Toggle to firm or client view. Drill-down available but never required to get value.

Firm Level & Client Level Views

Two views · one toggle

Two views serve two distinct operational contexts. Firm level shows aggregated data across all clients. Client level drills into a specific client, showing the same data filtered to that relationship. A single toggle keeps context clear and the transition fast.

Part Four · Develop · Rationale 03

One Category per Root Cause

Investigation has a starting point

I mapped each category to one of the three root cause types from research. Separating them makes the data immediately actionable: a team investigating a client complaint opens Client Analytics, a team investigating a recurring field error opens Field Analytics.

01

Client Analytics

Clients not meeting T+1, ranked by order volume. Top clients requiring manual intervention. Percentage of total firm orders. Makes client-side accountability visible and quantified.

Goes to client meetings

02

Field Analytics

Top amended fields by frequency, severity, and average resolution time. High-severity amendments broken out separately. Identifies which data-quality problems are most costly to resolve.

Order data · workflow signal

03

Service Analytics

Mismatched trades broken down by mismatch type across confirmation services: CTM (Central Trade Matching), AMS (Allocation Management System), and direct data feeds. Surfaces service-layer errors separately from client or field-level issues.

Internal · platform-level

Part Seven · Reflection

What I'd do differently: interviews earlier.

What I'd carry forward

Reflection 01

Domain knowledge is a design prerequisite.

The allocations workflow and service relationships had to be learned before any design decision could be grounded rather than guessed. On a project like this, that time isn't overhead. It is the work.

Reflection 02

Engage users earlier than feels necessary.

I set up interviews too late. Earlier outreach would have brought real user perspectives into scope decisions sooner and kept the work grounded from the start.
