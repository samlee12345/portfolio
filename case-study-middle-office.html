Overview

No scope, no problem statement, no existing design.

Fidessa's middle office operations teams — approximately 300,000 professionals across 600+ global financial firms — manage the post-trade settlement lifecycle. I was asked to improve the middle office workflow. There was no defined scope, no clear problem statement, and no existing design to build from.

I led the project end-to-end as the sole designer: from domain research and problem definition through discovery workshops, iterative design, and two rounds of validation with operations staff.

The Problem

No brief. No data. No way to be proactive.

Stated ask

Enhance the middle office workflow.

Actual problem

Operations teams had no historical or analytical view of their allocations. When settlement failed, there was no data to help staff identify patterns — which clients were causing delays, how often, or why. Without that visibility, teams couldn't be proactive: they couldn't make changes to their data management systems, and they couldn't tell clients to correct how they were sending information. Everything was reactive.

Why it matters: a regulatory change is driving urgency. Current settlement requirements mandate T+2 completion (within 2 business days of trade). In 2027, the requirement tightens to T+1 — cutting the allowable window in half. Teams that can't identify and address sources of delays before that change will be directly exposed to compliance risk.

Understanding the Domain

The settlement lifecycle is more complex than it looks.

Before making any design decisions, I needed to understand how settlement actually worked — the workflow, the data, the tools, and where things broke down.

I conducted extensive information transfer sessions with PMs to build domain fluency, then led cross-functional discovery workshops to map the end-to-end workflow across teams. The output: workflow maps and information maps covering all services and functionalities users currently interact with.

What surfaced: the settlement workflow has significant variation across client and account types. Each process variation displayed in its own grid, with no consolidated view. When errors occurred in Fidessa Data Manager, there was no front-end message explaining what was wrong or how to fix it. Critically, there was no historical data layer at all — teams had no way to see patterns over time, only the current state of their queue.

Defining the Scope

Five problem areas. One design vehicle.

The original ask gave me nothing to constrain against. Through discovery, I identified five distinct problem areas.

01

Dashboard

Fragmented views of the same workflow displayed across separate grids

02

Summary analytics

No data on which clients or accounts generate the most errors or require the most manual steps

03

Error handling

Unclear next steps when an issue occurs

04

Data Manager setup

Prerequisites for one-time entries were undocumented and invisible to users

05

Data Manager errors

Silent failures with no front-end direction on resolution

Nothing was cut from scope — the scope was large and remained large. What I determined through discovery was that a dashboard was the right vehicle to support all of it. Middle office users are expert users with deeply ingrained workflows. Any changes to how they currently work would create significant disruption. The dashboard approach added a new analytical layer without forcing changes to existing workflows — surfacing the information they needed without asking them to work differently.

Design Process

Tables, not charts. Default state, not configuration.

The core design challenge was visualization: how do you represent the health of thousands of client accounts and settlement batches in a way that lets an operations professional immediately see where to focus?

Middle office users are highly comfortable with data tables — they modify them, filter them, and navigate them fluently. The goal was not to replace tables with charts, but to design tables where the default state surfaces a clear next step. A user opening the dashboard should immediately see which clients are creating the most noise and know what to raise in a client conversation — without configuring anything first.

Decision 1

Consolidating fragmented workflow views into a single dashboard

The existing setup displayed each process variation in its own grid. The unified view lets operations staff see across workflow types in one place, without switching contexts or reconciling separate tools.

Decision 2

Surfacing data at the client and account level over time

To enable proactive management, the design presents aggregate error data historically — not just the current state of a queue. Summary views with drill-down let users see patterns: which clients are repeatedly causing delays, how many manual steps a given client typically requires, and where errors cluster.

Decision 3

Error state guidance

For Fidessa Data Manager silent failures, I designed a system to surface actionable direction when an error occurred, without over-engineering a solution for a rare edge case.

Validation

Two rounds. Round 1 confirmed the concept. Round 2 confirmed the copy.

Round 1 received positive reception across the board. Users immediately understood the value of historical analytical data — something that had never existed for them before.

"This is great! We've been waiting for a way to get historical analytical data about our allocations."

"I would definitely use this to find out what to talk to clients about."

Round 2 focused on copy validation — confirming that terminology and labels were appropriate across different regional teams, given that middle office workflows and naming conventions can vary by market.

Reflection

Domain first. Design second.

This project required significant domain learning before any design work could start. The settlement lifecycle, the relationships between services, and how allocations are managed at the client and account level were not things I could assume going in — they had to be learned from the ground up.

What I'd do differently: be more proactive about engaging users earlier. Setting up interviews took longer than it should have. Earlier outreach would have compressed the discovery phase and brought in real perspectives sooner.
