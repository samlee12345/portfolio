Overview

Brief pushback. A narrow brief, a misframed problem. The brief: alert on a single order. The reality: users needed alerts across groups, and couldn't discover functionality that already existed.

Fidessa's alerting functionality serves every persona on the platform: front office traders, middle office operations staff, sales desks, and more. I was asked to extend alerting from filter grids to individual orders.

What began as a single-order alert became a full redesign. Users needed alerts across order groups to match their pace, and couldn't find functionality that already existed because it was scattered across the platform.

I led the project end-to-end as the sole designer, conducting knowledge transfer sessions and in-depth user interviews across all major personas, and delivered two versions: an additive release that shipped, and a comprehensive redesign consolidating all alerting into a single hub.

The Problem

The brief was too narrow. The real problem was structural.

Stated ask

Allow users to create alerts for specific thresholds on individual orders. The original system only supported alerts on filter tables. The ask was to extend this to order-level metadata: time-based alerts, apply-to-all-orders, and market movement alerts.

Actual problem

The original scope was narrow enough to ship, but it wasn't addressing why so few users engaged with alerts in the first place. The deeper issue was fragmentation: alerting capability was scattered across multiple tools, making the system hard to discover, configure, and trust.

13% Actively using alerts
66% Wanted more, couldn't navigate it
13% Had alerts, were ignoring them

Why it matters: for front office traders, a well-configured alert replaces manual monitoring. For operations staff, alerts surface issues before they escalate. When the system is too fragmented to navigate coherently, users stop using it. The 13% active usage rate was the evidence.

Understanding the Domain

66% of users wanted alerts but couldn't navigate the system.

The complexity here was persona complexity, not technical complexity. Alerts mean different things to different users.

Sales Trader

Client-facing. Needs time-based alerts tied to specific client orders and execution windows.

High-Touch Trader

Manages individual orders with direct client involvement. Alert on individual order metadata.

Low-Touch Trader

High volume, algorithmic execution. Needs apply-to-all and market movement, not time-based client alerts.

Knowledge transfer sessions with SMEs produced a functionality map covering all alerting behavior and current state across the platform. In-depth user interviews across all three persona types followed, with research synthesis identifying shared needs, persona-specific needs, and gaps.

Key finding: many users weren't aware of the full range of alerting capabilities that already existed. The problem wasn't only missing functionality: the existing functionality was too fragmented to discover and configure reliably.

Defining the Scope

Additive improvements don't fix fragmentation.

I pushed back on the additive scope after research made clear it addressed the symptom, not the cause. Adding new alert types to a fragmented system would give users more to navigate without making the system easier to use.

Design Process

One hub. Three personas. No separate flows.

The core challenge was building one system that served three personas with different alerting needs, without fragmenting the experience back into persona-specific flows, which was the original problem.

Decision 1

Unified architecture, not persona-specific flows

One hub for all personas and all alert types. Persona-specific differences are handled through what's available to configure, not through separate interfaces.

Decision 2

New alert types driven by what validation revealed

Round 1 validation confirmed order-level alerts were valuable and surfaced that they weren't enough. That finding directly drove the scope expansion into time-based, apply-to-all, and market movement alerts, validated in round 2.

Reflection

Push back before designs exist, not after.

The original scope of this project was too narrow. The brief was defined before anyone had talked to users about why alert adoption was so low. Continuing with that scope would have meant building something that made the problem incrementally better without fixing it.

The most important move was making that case early, before designs existed. The research gave me the evidence, and the willingness to push back on scope changed what the project became.
