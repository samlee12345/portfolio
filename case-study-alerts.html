Part One

Project Overview

The Mission

Fidessa is an enterprise order management system (OMS) used by 600+ trading firms, where front office traders manage and monitor client orders at volume. With only 13% of traders using alerts and triggers limited to order state changes, not the performance fields traders actually watch, there was a clear opportunity to redesign alerting to fit how front office traders work.

Key Responsibilities

  • End-to-end ownership across research, design, and release
  • Domain research and functionality mapping across the platform
  • User research and validation across three front office trader personas
  • Built the Fidessa Alerts Concept Library: reusable alert components

Why Do Users Need Better Alerting?

Front office traders are legally required to take all reasonable steps to secure the most favorable terms for every client order. That obligation requires effective order monitoring, but current alerts fire on order state changes, not on the performance data traders use to decide whether to intervene. Manual monitoring doesn’t scale.

  • Sales traders: client relationships at risk. Without timely alerts on fill progress, they can't advise clients on next steps or demonstrate they pursued best execution.
  • High-touch traders: missed execution timing windows.
  • Low-touch traders: no early signal on algo performance. By the time underperformance is visible, it has already compounded across the full order set.

Better alerting closes that gap, letting the system flag what traders can't watch, across every order, simultaneously.

The Problem

Front office traders monitor 100–10,000 orders at any moment with no automated way to flag which need attention. The only fallback is filtered grids. Adoption sat at 13%, with most users having inherited alerts from a colleague. Triggers were limited to order state changes, not the performance fields traders actually watch. A new regional client also required single-order alerting as a contract condition.

Stated Ask

Allow alerting on single orders and alert triggering on order metadata.

User Sentiment

  • Unmet needs: alerts don't support the trigger types or workflows front office traders rely on
  • Poor discoverability: traders don't know how to configure alerts or what functionality already exists on the platform
  • Competitive frustration: comparable trading platforms offer threshold and performance-based alerting as standard; Fidessa does not

Business Impact

  • Negative impression: front office traders are the highest-value user segment, and a tool that doesn't fit their workflow is a visible failure
  • Competitive gap: alternative trading platforms offer threshold alerting as standard; Fidessa does not
  • Productivity loss: time spent monitoring is time not spent executing. Ineffective alerting makes that trade-off unavoidable

Design Decisions

01Notification Delivery Labels

Hypothesis

Existing labels described system behavior, not outcome. "Flash tab" triggered a hidden second behavior. Reframing around delivery destination would reduce misconfiguration.

Variations explored

  • Tooltips on existing labels
  • Plain language rename ("Highlight tab," "Bring window to front")
  • "Display location" radio group: Alerts toolbar / New toolbar button / Notifications grid
  • Sound off by default

Insight

Traders think in terms of where, not how. "Display location" radio group was the clearest framing. Sound retained but unchecked by default.

03Alert Creation: Single Order vs Group of Orders

Hypothesis

Single order requires confirming the exact target. Group requires understanding scope. One modal serving both would over-inform one and under-inform the other.

Variations explored

  • Two distinct dialogs

Two dialogs. Single shows the full order metadata string. Group shows count, name, and active filters. Simplified group notifications removed a decision that didn't need to be made.

04Alert Discovery on the Order Row

Hypothesis

Once set, traders need passive visibility that an alert exists without opening a separate panel. An indicator on the order row would surface alert status at a glance.

Variations explored

  • Full row color highlight: too heavy, conflicts with existing states
  • Icon on an existing column: inconsistent scanning
  • Dedicated Alert column (bell icon) + Alert triggered time column
  • Status bar confirmation at creation

Insight

Two columns separate the two states that matter: has-alert and has-fired. Status bar message at creation confirms the action without interrupting flow.

05Quick Alert Button

Hypothesis

High-touch traders set the same alert type on dozens of orders daily. A pre-configurable toolbar button would reduce repeated setup. Key question: silent apply or pre-fill with editability?

Variations explored

  • Fixed button: opens full dialog from scratch every time
  • One-click silent apply: no opportunity to adjust
  • Pre-configurable via Toolbar Builder, fields editable at use
  • Multiple buttons per alert type

Insight

Pre-configured defaults, fully editable at use. Traders adjust thresholds per order, so removing editability would reduce usefulness. Toolbar Builder reused a known pattern.

06Notifications

Hypothesis

Existing signals gave no context. Traders knew something fired, not what or why. The underlying need was a to-do list: a centralized view with enough order context to act immediately.

Variations explored

  • Toast per alert: no history retained
  • Count badge only: no way to review past alerts
  • Full blotter with type, condition, client, instrument, timestamp
  • Inline detail panel on row selection, no double-click required

Insight

Row selection surfaces full order detail and action buttons inline. No separate window, no double-click. "Applied to" filter keeps the blotter manageable at scale.

Design Decisions

01Layout Anatomy

The Create Alert dialog was structured into three distinct sections, each serving a different decision in the alert setup flow.

Order details

Read-only context confirming what the alert is applied to. For a single order: instrument, side, quantity, algo, counterparty, and timestamp. For a group: group name, active filters, and order count. The trader verifies the target before configuring anything.

Trigger conditions

Where the trader defines what should fire the alert. A three-part row (field, operator, value) keeps the interaction compact. The field dropdown is curated to the most relevant order metadata, not the full data model.

Notification settings

Where the alert will appear when it fires. Separated from trigger conditions because the two decisions are independent: what fires the alert has nothing to do with where you see it. Structured around delivery destination rather than system behavior.

01Alert Scope Expansion

Hypothesis

Single-order alerts weren't enough. Traders needed to alert on all orders in a security and on the security itself. Three different scopes needed one single entry point.

  • Scope selector dropdown in the modal header
  • Segmented tab control, consistent trigger UI below
  • Scope-sensitive context header (order metadata vs. live market data)

Insight

Segmented tabs. Each surfaces scope-relevant context in the header while the trigger builder stays consistent. All orders handled as a fourth independent scope.

02Expanded Trigger Library

Hypothesis

Order metadata covered execution quality but left traders blind to market and macro conditions. The expanded library needed to serve four scopes without creating four separate builders.

Variations explored

  • Fully separate lists per scope: four builders, high complexity

03Unified Alert Hub

Hypothesis

Alerting was scattered with no central entry point. Traders couldn't find, manage, or review what had fired. System and user-created alerts needed to live in one place with inline editing.

Variations explored

  • Standalone alerts panel as a separate window
  • Embedded tab within the existing toolbar grid
  • Two-panel layout: alert list left, settings right
  • Pre-defined alerts grouped separately with inline fired counts
  • Multi-condition AND logic: deferred from Round 1, included here

Insight

Two-panel layout. Pre-defined and user-defined grouped separately. Fired counts surface previously hidden system alerts. AND logic included. Inline editing throughout.

04Triggered Notifications Grid

Hypothesis

Traders need to act on a fired alert without losing their place. A dedicated grid with inline order detail would let them respond without navigating away.

Variations explored

  • Toast or banner only: no persistent record
  • Alerts surfaced back in the hub: conflates management with response
  • Dedicated notifications grid with inline detail and action buttons
  • Order actions available directly from the grid

Insight

Dedicated grid with Acknowledge, Snooze, Manage actions and full order detail inline. Order actions available without navigating to the blotter. Keeps alert creation and alert response as distinct workflows.

The Problem

The brief was too narrow. The real problem was structural.

Current state

Alerts were set on filtered grids. Filtering was limited to order states (assigned person, outlier state, status), not performance thresholds. Only 13% of users were actively using alerting.

Original ask

Allow users to create alerts at the single-order level with the ability to trigger on order-level thresholds (fill percentage, price level, order volume done).

Understanding the Domain

Every persona. Every workflow stage. The domain research had to cover both depth and breadth.

Knowledge transfer sessions with SMEs produced a functionality map covering all alerting behavior and current state across the platform. In-depth user interviews across all three persona types followed, with research synthesis identifying shared needs, persona-specific needs, and gaps.

Key finding: many users weren't aware of the full range of alerting capabilities that already existed. The problem wasn't only missing functionality: the existing functionality was too fragmented to discover and configure reliably.

Research Findings

Actual problem

The original scope was narrow enough to ship, but it wasn't addressing why so few users engaged with alerts in the first place. User interviews across all three persona types surfaced a consistent pattern: traders weren't avoiding alerts by choice. The trigger conditions were limited to order state changes, which didn't map to how traders actually monitor their work. The deeper issue was twofold: the one-order-at-a-time model couldn't keep up with the volume of orders traders monitor, and alerting functionality was spread across tools with no single place to manage it, leaving most users unaware of what the platform already offered, including timing and macro triggers (time-based and market movement) that already existed but couldn't be found.

Reflection

A hunch without evidence doesn't win the argument.

Reflection 1

Instinct without evidence isn't enough to push back

Before design round 1, there was an inkling the scope was too narrow. Senior colleagues advised holding off. Without the research to back it, there wasn't standing to push further. Once user interviews gave something concrete, the scope changed. But that came a round later than it could have. Instinct tells you where to look. Evidence is what lets you push.

Reflection 2

Domain research time is design time

Month 1 was entirely knowledge transfer. On a fast-moving project that can feel like lost time. Here it was the opposite. Understanding the full alert landscape across the platform was what made it possible to identify fragmentation as a possible root problem, not just missing features.
