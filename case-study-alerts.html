Part Two · Discover

User Research

3 personas · Floor walk + interviews

I ran research across all three front office personas: remote interviews and an on-site floor walk at a client trading desk, observing traders using the platform in real conditions.

Sales Trader

"My clients expect to hear from me throughout the day: where their order is, how it's tracking. If I'm not on top of that, the relationship suffers."

Alerting need

Fill progress alerts on client orders to communicate order status before clients ask.

Current gap

No fill rate (percentage of an order that has executed) alerts. Manual monitoring means delayed client updates.

High-Touch Trader

"Each order needs individual attention, but I'm managing hundreds. There's no way to set a threshold and get notified when an order needs me."

Alerting need

Order-metadata threshold alerts on individual orders: fill rate, price level, volume done.

Current gap

Triggers limited to order status changes. Performance data not alertable.

Low-Touch Trader

"If I could get an alert when an order starts underperforming before it errors out, I could adjust the routing rule proactively instead of cleaning up the damage after."

Alerting need

Algo-level threshold alerts. All orders routed to an algo (algorithmic trading strategy) feed into one aggregate metric.

Current gap

Single-order model doesn't fit DMA (direct market access, automated order routing) volume. No early signal on algo performance before exceptions compound.

Part Two · Discover

Research Findings

Low adoption wasn't disinterest

Low adoption wasn't disinterest. The system simply wasn't built for how traders actually worked.

13%

Actively using alerts

13% active usage reflects users who had inherited their alert setups. False positives trained them to ignore notifications rather than engage with them.

66%

Wanted more, didn't know it existed

66% of users couldn't describe what alerting options were available. Functionality was fragmented across surfaces with no central entry point.

79%

Triggers were too limited

79% cited missing trigger types as the reason they stopped trying. Fill rate, price level, and volume done were not alertable at all.

Part Two · Discover

Key Insights

What the research said

I concluded that Fidessa's alert system wasn't built for front office volume. Alerts fired on errors after the fact rather than preventing them. With 100 to 10,000 orders per trader, the system needed to replace active monitoring entirely.

01

Alerts beyond filters

Traders need to alert on individual orders, not just filtered grid views. The existing model tied alerting to grid filters, not to the orders themselves.

02

Order data thresholds

Fill rate, price level, and volume done are the fields traders watch to decide when to act. None of them were alertable.

03

A single hub

Alerting functionality existed across multiple tools with no central entry point. Traders couldn't find it, so they didn't use it.

04

Invisible by design

Alerting was spread across multiple tools with no single place to manage it. Most users had no clear picture of what the platform already offered.

Part Three · Define

Decision 01 · Reframe the Brief

Bigger than the brief

The brief had a commercial condition: prospective clients required threshold alerting (alerts that fire when a numeric value crosses a set level, like "notify me when fill rate drops below 50%") before signing. My research showed the real problem was bigger. Fragmented alerting, limited trigger types, users who couldn't find what already existed. I argued for a full hub redesign, but that wasn't feasible within three months. The compromise I landed on: widen to multi-order alerts with threshold triggers within the existing architecture.

Original ask

Single-order alerts with threshold triggers, required by prospective clients in a new region as a condition of signing on.

Round 1 scope

Multi-order alerts with threshold triggers, within existing architecture. The prospective clients didn't sign. With the deadline gone, development proceeded with the full redesign.

Notification Labels

Where, not how

Hypothesis

Existing labels described system behavior, not outcome. "Flash tab" triggered a hidden second behavior. Reframing around delivery destination would reduce misconfiguration.

Insight

Traders think in terms of where, not how. "Display location" as a radio group was the clearest framing. Sound was retained but unchecked by default.

Decision 03 · Single Order vs Group

Two dialogs

Hypothesis

One modal serving both single orders and groups would over-inform one context and under-inform the other. Two distinct dialogs matched to each scope.

Insight

Single shows full order metadata. Group shows count, name, and active filters. Confirmed in testing as the right split.

Part Three · Define

Decision 04 · Alert Discovery on the Order Row

Passive visibility

Hypothesis

Once set, traders need passive visibility that an alert exists without opening a separate panel. An indicator on the order row would surface alert status at a glance.

Insight

Two columns separate the two states that matter: has-alert and has-fired. Status bar message at creation confirms without interrupting flow.

15
Part Three · Define

Decision 05 · Quick Alert Button

Defaults, editable

Hypothesis

A one-click entry point with pre-configured defaults would reduce friction at the moment of creation, when traders are mid-workflow on an order.

Insight

Pre-configured defaults, fully editable at use. Traders adjust thresholds per order, so removing editability would reduce usefulness.

16
Part Three · Define

Decision 06 · Notifications

Inline action, no modal

Hypothesis

Traders need to act on a fired alert without navigating away. Row selection surfacing full order detail inline would remove the need for a separate window.

Insight

Row selection surfaces full order detail and action buttons inline. No separate window, no double-click. "Applied to" filter keeps the blotter manageable at scale.

Reflection

What the project proved

Reflection 01

Audit the brief before designing, not after.

Single-order alerting was likely a symptom of a broader monitoring gap, not the root problem. I couldn't interview the prospective clients directly. They hadn't signed and the negotiation calls weren't recorded. By the time I had the research to push back, a round had already been designed. Today I'd insert myself into those conversations earlier and validate the brief before a line of design starts.

Reflection 02

Impact moved UX from downstream to upstream.

After delivering the 34x increase, PMs and the Head of Equities started bringing me into roadmap discussions before requirements were written. I also initiated proactive floor walks to build a reservoir of user context independent of any active project, so when timelines compressed and research had to be cut, I had something to draw from. The metric wasn't just the outcome. It was the credibility to get involved earlier.
