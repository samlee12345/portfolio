Part One

Project Overview

The Mission

Fidessa is an enterprise order management system built for trading firms. Across 600+ firms, sales traders, high-touch/low-touch traders, and middle office traders use it to manage and monitor client orders at volume.

Front office traders already know which fields tell them whether an order is progressing as expected: fill rate, price level, volume done. The gap was that the alert system couldn't trigger on those fields. With only 13% of traders using alerts and triggers limited to order state changes, traders were monitoring those fields manually instead. There was a clear opportunity to redesign alerting to better support how front office traders actually manage their orders.

Key Responsibilities

  • End-to-end ownership across research, design, and release
  • Domain research and functionality mapping across the platform
  • User research and validation across 3 front office trader personas
  • Built the Fidessa Alerts Concept Library: reusable alert components

The Problem

The project had an additional commercial driver: prospective clients in a new region required single-order alerting as a condition of signing on. Traders in that region manage significantly higher order volumes, making system-driven alerting a workflow requirement rather than a preference.

Front office traders monitor between 500 and 10,000 orders a day, depending on their role, with no automated way to flag which ones need attention. Without proper alerting, the only fallback is filtered grids. Adoption sat at 13%, with most active users having inherited their alerts from a previous user or having them configured by the firm. Alert triggers are limited to order state changes, not the performance fields traders actually watch.

User Sentiment

  • Unmet needs: alerts don't support the trigger types or workflows front office traders rely on
  • Poor discoverability: traders don't know how to configure alerts or what functionality already exists on the platform
  • Competitive frustration: comparable trading platforms offer threshold and performance-based alerting as standard; Fidessa does not

Business Impact

  • Negative impression: front office traders are the highest-value user segment, and a tool that doesn't fit their workflow is a visible failure
  • Competitive gap: alternative trading platforms offer threshold alerting as standard; Fidessa does not
  • Productivity loss: manual monitoring across 500 to 10,000 orders per trader represents significant desk time that proper alerting would eliminate

The Solution

Two versions delivered: one built for the timeline, one built for the users.

Addressing User Needs

  • Fit for purpose: meets the needs of all three front office trader personas
  • Full coverage: supports single-order, multi-order, and apply-to-all alert configurations across all three trader personas
  • Threshold control: traders define alerts based on the conditions they are currently monitoring to evaluate order performance

Why Do Users Need Better Alerting?

Front office traders are legally required to take all reasonable steps to secure the most favorable terms for every client order. That obligation requires effective order monitoring, but current alerts don't support it. Existing triggers fire on order state changes, not on the performance data traders use to decide whether to intervene, leaving traders to monitor manually. Manual monitoring doesn't scale:

  • Sales traders: client relationships at risk -- without timely alerts on fill progress, they can't advise clients on whether to act, and can't demonstrate they pursued best execution on their behalf.
  • High-touch traders: missed execution timing windows.
  • Low-touch traders: no early signal on algo performance -- by the time underperformance is visible, it has already compounded across the full order set.

Better alerting closes that gap, letting the system flag what traders can't watch, across every order, simultaneously.

My Impact

Two design deliverables: an additive release constrained by the initial client timeline, and a full hub redesign. When the India team didn't sign and the timeline pressure lifted, the full redesign is what shipped.

Project Impact

  • Exceeding KPI expectations: 25x increase in alerts created within 12 months, surpassing the 20x target set by the head of product.
  • Competitive parity: 600+ firms gained threshold alerting on par with alternative trading platforms, closing a capability gap that had been a consistent source of competitive frustration.
  • UX precedent: first UX-led project at Fidessa to ship. Developer collaboration throughout ensured technical feasibility where a previous UX effort had not, establishing a process that subsequent projects followed.

Organisational Influence

  • UX as a practice: established UX involvement as a standard part of the product development process at Fidessa, shifting the team from shipping without user input to treating research and validation as a prerequisite for scope decisions.
  • UX as a partner: pushed for and shipped the full hub redesign, unprompted, turning user research findings into a live product rather than a scope compromise.
  • Consolidated alert architecture: the full hub redesign replaces alerting functionality scattered across multiple tools with a single entry point, reducing code duplication and giving the platform a maintainable foundation for future alert development.

Alert fatigue

User interviews surfaced a compounding problem: many traders had alerts enabled that they had inherited from previous traders, or that their firm had configured during onboarding. Over time, repeated false positives and triggers for conditions they no longer cared about trained them to ignore all alerts, including ones they had deliberately set up themselves. Low adoption wasn't only about discoverability. The system had eroded trust in the mechanism entirely. Fixing the tool wasn't enough. The redesign also had to rebuild the expectation that an alert was worth acting on.

Current state: trader workflow

Key Insights

Alerts beyond filters

Traders need to alert on individual orders, not just filtered grid views. The existing model tied alerting to grid filters, not to the orders themselves.

Order data thresholds

Fill rate, price level, and volume done are the fields traders watch to decide when to act. None of them were alertable.

A single hub

Alerting functionality existed across multiple tools with no central entry point. Traders couldn't find it, so they didn't use it.

Alerts at Volume

The existing alerting system was built on a specific design assumption: alerts are a last resort. They fire when an order has already entered an error or outlier state, and by that point the system is confirming damage, not preventing it. That model is coherent if traders are actively watching their orders and need a signal only when something exceptional happens.

It breaks completely when a trader is managing hundreds to thousands of orders simultaneously. At that scale, manual monitoring isn't a strategy. You cannot watch every order. The alert has to replace active monitoring, not supplement it. A system that only fires on errors doesn't help a trader decide when to act. It tells them they've already missed the window.

Part Three

Define

The original brief was commercially driven. Research said the problem was bigger.

Redefining the Scope

The brief came with a commercial condition: prospective clients in a new region required single-order alerts with threshold triggers before they would sign on. The business was aiming to close those contracts within three months.

Research confirmed the fuller problem. Knowledge transfer sessions and user interviews showed fragmented alerting across the platform, trigger types limited to order state changes, no support for market movement triggers, and users who couldn't find functionality that already existed. The right answer was a full hub redesign.

That wasn't feasible from a development standpoint within three months. The compromise: widen from single-order to multi-order alerts and add threshold triggers, within the existing architecture. Multi-order was a real improvement on the original brief, identified by user interviews. It wasn't the UX ideal, but it was a compromise that had to be made to meet the contract deadline. The prospective clients ultimately didn't sign. With the timeline constraint gone, development proceeded with the full redesign.

Design Decisions

01Notification Delivery Labels

Hypothesis

The existing notification settings described internal system behavior rather than user outcome. "Flash tab" flashed the grid tab, but also flashed any toolbar button the user had configured for that alert, two distinct delivery behaviors bundled under one option with no indication of the second. "Display control" opened the originating grid. "Set focus to control" set focus to it. The distinction between the last two was invisible at setup. Reframing options around where a notification appears, rather than what the system does internally, would reduce misconfiguration and help traders make an informed choice on first use.

Variations explored

  • Keep existing labels, add tooltips explaining each option
  • Rename to plain language: "Highlight tab," "Bring window to front"
  • "Display location" grouping with three delivery destinations as radio buttons: Alerts toolbar button / Create new toolbar button / Notifications grid
  • Sound options unchecked by default. Floor walk sessions confirmed sound alerts would trigger too frequently on a trading floor to be useful

Current state

Redesigned

Insight

Traders think about where they want to see a result, not what the system does. The "Display location" radio group was the clearest framing. Sound was retained as an option but unchecked by default. For group alerts, notification settings were simplified to a plain-text statement since the delivery path is fixed and the configuration panel added no value.

02Trigger Condition Fields

Hypothesis

The existing system only supported pre-defined system event categories with no user-defined thresholds. Traders needed to define conditions based on live order performance data. A curated set of the most relevant fields, presented as a structured three-part row, would be more usable than exposing the full data model.

Variations explored

  • Free-text condition entry: too error-prone for a fast-moving trading environment
  • Full list of all configurable data fields: too many options, no clear starting point
  • Curated dropdown ordered by frequency of use: Fill percentage, Price level, Index level, Last traded quantity, Order volume done, Order volume remaining, Order off limit for, Reporting quantity
  • AND/OR multi-condition logic: considered and deferred. Adding it in Round 1 would have significantly increased dialog complexity without a validated need

Insight

A curated dropdown with a three-part row (field / operator / value + unit) was the right level of complexity for Round 1. Even a single threshold condition was a net-new capability over the current state. Multi-condition AND/OR logic was a conscious deferral, not an oversight.

03Alert Creation: Single Order vs Group

Hypothesis

What a trader needs to confirm before creating an alert differs between a single order and a group. For a single order, they need to verify the exact order. For a group, they need to understand the scope of what they're alerting on. A single modal trying to serve both contexts would over-inform one or under-inform the other.

Variations explored

  • Single modal that adapts its header based on source type
  • Two distinct dialogs with different header sections
  • Group dialog: surface group metadata (name, active filters, order count) instead of order-level fields
  • Group dialog: simplified notification settings as a plain-text statement, since delivery for group alerts is fixed
  • Multi-selected orders (not a saved group): considered as a third case, resolved by creating individual alerts per selected order rather than a temporary unnamed group

Insight

Two distinct dialogs. The single order dialog shows the full order metadata string so the trader can confirm the exact order. The group dialog shows how many orders are in scope, the group name, and active filters, giving the trader visibility into the alert's reach before committing. Reducing group notification settings to a text statement removed a configuration decision that didn't need to be made.

04Alert Discovery on the Order Row

Hypothesis

Once an alert is set, traders need passive visibility that it exists without opening a separate panel. An indicator directly on the order row would let traders see which orders have alerts and whether those alerts have already fired, with no additional navigation required.

Variations explored

  • Full row color highlight when an alert is active: too visually heavy, conflicts with existing row states
  • Icon appended to an existing column: harder to scan consistently across orders
  • Dedicated "Alert" column with bell icon, plus a separate "Alert triggered time" column
  • Status bar confirmation message at the moment of creation

Insight

Two dedicated columns (Alert bell icon and Alert triggered time) separated the two states that matter: does this order have an alert set, and has it fired? A status bar message at creation confirmed the action in context without interrupting the trader's flow.

05Quick Alert Button

Hypothesis

High-touch traders set the same alert type on dozens of orders throughout the day. Creating an alert from scratch every time was too slow. A pre-configurable toolbar button (with condition type, operator, and threshold value saved once) would reduce repeated setup overhead. The key design question was whether clicking the button should apply the alert silently, or pre-fill the dialog and keep all fields editable before confirming.

Variations explored

  • Fixed "Set up alert" button with no pre-configuration: opens the full dialog from scratch every time
  • One-click silent apply: button applies saved defaults with no confirmation step or opportunity to adjust
  • Pre-configurable button through the existing Toolbar Builder, with defaults pre-filled but all fields editable at time of use
  • Multiple distinct buttons per saved alert type

Insight

Pre-configured defaults with full reconfigurability at time of use. When a trader clicks the quick alert button, the Create Alert dialog opens with the condition type, operator, and value pre-filled, but every field remains editable. Traders needed to adjust threshold values for different orders, so removing editability would have made the button faster but far less useful. Users prioritize flexibility. The Toolbar Builder integration required no new UI paradigm. Traders already knew the pattern.

06Notifications

Hypothesis

The existing notification mechanism gave traders a signal but no context. For user-defined alerts, traders knew something had fired from the toolbar button dropdown and a highlighted row on the grid, but not which alert or what condition was met. System alerts surfaced the alert name and triggering order, but delivery was still limited to a toolbar blink. Neither path gave traders a complete picture: what fired, why, and what to do next.

The underlying need was a to-do list. Traders wanted to use alerts to queue orders for action, not just receive a passive signal. A centralized triggered alerts view with enough order context to act immediately was the missing piece.

Variations explored

  • Toast notification per alert, dismissed individually, no history retained
  • Count badge on the toolbar button only: no way to review past alerts
  • Full blotter with Type, Alert condition, Client, Instrument, and triggered timestamp columns
  • Detail panel below the blotter surfacing order context and action buttons on row selection, with no double-click required to open order details

Insight

Selecting a triggered alert row surfaces the full order detail and action buttons immediately in the panel below, with no separate window and no double-click. Order actions (Amend, Multi-split, Complete, Fills) are available directly from the notification. On a trading floor, removing that one extra step matters. The "Applied to" filter at the top keeps the blotter manageable as alert volume scales.

Design Decisions

01Layout Anatomy

The Create Alert dialog was structured into three distinct sections, each serving a different decision in the alert setup flow.

Order details

Read-only context confirming what the alert is applied to. For a single order: instrument, side, quantity, algo, counterparty, and timestamp. For a group: group name, active filters, and order count. The trader verifies the target before configuring anything.

Trigger conditions

Where the trader defines what should fire the alert. A three-part row (field, operator, value) keeps the interaction compact. The field dropdown is curated to the most relevant order metadata, not the full data model.

Notification settings

Where the alert will appear when it fires. Separated from trigger conditions because the two decisions are independent: what fires the alert has nothing to do with where you see it. Structured around delivery destination rather than system behavior.

Redevelop

Three changes. One unified system.

The full redesign extended alerting beyond individual orders to support three new scopes, expanded the trigger library to include market and time-based conditions, and consolidated all alerting into a single hub.

Design Decisions

01Alert Scope Expansion

Hypothesis

The original design only supported alerts on individual orders. Traders also needed to alert on all their orders in a security and on the security itself as a market instrument. Three fundamentally different scopes needed to be accessible from a single entry point without requiring traders to navigate to separate tools.

Variations explored

  • Separate entry points for each scope type: three different modal triggers from different UI surfaces
  • A scope selector dropdown within the existing modal header
  • A segmented tab control at the top of the modal, switching context while keeping the same trigger condition UI below
  • Scope-sensitive context header: order metadata string for order-level, live market data (price, spread, vol) for security-level

Insight

Segmented tabs. Each tab surfaces context relevant to that scope in the header — order metadata for single orders, live security data for market-level alerts — while the trigger condition builder stays consistent across all three. A fourth scope, All orders, handles portfolio-level conditions independently.

Security and all orders for security

Portfolio-level (all orders)

02Expanded Trigger Library

Hypothesis

Order metadata fields covered execution quality but left traders blind to market conditions and time-based factors. The expanded library needed to serve four different alert scopes without overwhelming traders with irrelevant options — and without creating four separate condition builders.

Variations explored

  • One universal field list across all scopes: too many irrelevant options per scope, harder to scan
  • Fully separate field lists per scope: four condition builders, increased design and development complexity
  • Shared fields for order-level scopes with a distinct set for market-level: reduces cognitive load while covering all use cases

Insight

Order, All orders of a security, and All orders share the same field library. Market Level Security has its own distinct set. One condition builder pattern, two field sets — scoped by whichever tab the trader has selected.

03Unified Alert Hub

Hypothesis

Alerting functionality existed across multiple tools with no central entry point. Traders couldn't find what was available, couldn't manage what they'd created, and had no visibility into what had already fired. A single hub needed to surface both system alerts and user-created alerts in one place, with inline editing.

Variations explored

  • Standalone alerts panel as a separate window
  • Embedded alerts tab within the existing toolbar grid
  • Two-panel layout: alert list on the left, settings panel on the right
  • Pre-defined system alerts grouped separately from user-created alerts, with fired counts visible inline
  • Multi-condition support (Add condition): deferred from Round 1, included here

Insight

Two-panel layout with pre-defined and user-defined alerts grouped separately. Pre-defined alerts surface system functionality that previously required traders to know where to look — the counts (31 rejected orders, 146 risk alerts) make unfired alerts immediately visible. User-defined alerts are listed below with inline editing. Multi-condition AND logic was included in this release, deferred from Round 1.

04Triggered Notifications Grid

Hypothesis

Traders need to act on a fired alert without losing their place. A dedicated notifications grid should surface triggered alerts with full order detail inline, so traders can acknowledge, snooze, or respond to an order without navigating to a separate surface.

Variations explored

  • Inline toast or banner notification only: no persistent record, fired alerts disappear
  • Triggered alerts surfaced back inside the hub: conflates alert management with alert response
  • Dedicated notifications grid with inline order detail and action buttons
  • Order actions (Amend, Multi-split, Complete) available directly from the grid without navigating to the blotter

Insight

Dedicated grid with three notification actions — Acknowledge, Snooze, Manage — and full order detail shown in a panel below the selected row. Traders can complete order actions (Amend, Multi-split, Complete) directly from the grid, keeping the response within one surface. Separating the notifications grid from the hub keeps alert creation and alert response as distinct workflows.

Accessibility

Alerts that can't be missed. By anyone.

Front office trading is a high-pressure, time-critical environment. Accessibility considerations were scoped around three areas where failure has real consequences: cognitive load during peak activity, visual signaling under stress, and keyboard navigability for power users who never leave the keyboard.

Color independence

Alert states use icon, label, and color together, never color as the sole indicator. Tested against protanopia and deuteranopia simulations to ensure state is always readable.

Keyboard navigation

Alert creation, editing, and dismissal flows are fully keyboard-navigable. Power users who live in the keyboard shouldn't need to break context to manage an alert.

Cognitive load

Triggered notifications can be acknowledged to confirm they've been seen, or snoozed to defer action without dismissing them. Traders can clear the desk during peak activity without losing track of what fired.

Overview

Brief pushback. A narrow brief, a misframed problem. The brief: alert on a single order. The reality: users needed alerts on multiple orders, and couldn't discover functionality that already existed.

Fidessa's alerting functionality serves front office traders across the platform: sales traders, high-touch traders, and low-touch traders. I was tasked to extend alerting from filter grids to individual orders.

User interviews killed the original scope. Single-order alerts wouldn't address the actual pain points: users needed alerts on multiple orders to match their fast-paced work style, and a single alerting hub to fix the discoverability problem caused by functionality scattered across tools.

I led the project end-to-end as the sole designer, conducting knowledge transfer sessions, design iterations, and in-depth user interviews across all major personas. The project produced two design rounds: an additive release scoped to a client contract deadline, and a comprehensive redesign consolidating all alerting into a single hub. When the India team didn't sign and the timeline constraint lifted, the full redesign is what shipped.

The Problem

The brief was too narrow. The real problem was structural.

Current state

Alerts were set on filtered grids. Filtering was limited to order states (assigned person, outlier state, status), not performance thresholds. Only 13% of users were actively using alerting.

Original ask

Allow users to create alerts at the single-order level with the ability to trigger on order-level thresholds (fill percentage, price level, order volume done).

Understanding the Domain

Every persona. Every workflow stage. The domain research had to cover both depth and breadth.

Knowledge transfer sessions with SMEs produced a functionality map covering all alerting behavior and current state across the platform. In-depth user interviews across all three persona types followed, with research synthesis identifying shared needs, persona-specific needs, and gaps.

Key finding: many users weren't aware of the full range of alerting capabilities that already existed. The problem wasn't only missing functionality: the existing functionality was too fragmented to discover and configure reliably.

Research Findings

Low adoption wasn't disinterest. It was a system that didn't fit how traders work.

Actual problem

The original scope was narrow enough to ship, but it wasn't addressing why so few users engaged with alerts in the first place. User interviews across all three persona types surfaced a consistent pattern: traders weren't avoiding alerts by choice. The trigger conditions were limited to order state changes, which didn't map to how traders actually monitor their work. The deeper issue was twofold: the one-order-at-a-time model couldn't keep up with the volume of orders traders monitor, and alerting functionality was spread across tools with no single place to manage it, leaving most users unaware of what the platform already offered, including timing and macro triggers (time-based and market movement) that already existed but couldn't be found.

66% Wanted more, didn't know functionalities already existed

Why it matters: for front office traders, a well-configured alert replaces manual monitoring. State-based alerts tell you what already happened. Traders need signals earlier. Fill rate and volume done tell them whether to rework a strategy before an order reaches a problem state. The 13% active usage rate was the evidence.

Redefining the Scope

Additive improvements don't fix fragmentation.

The original brief came with a commercial deadline: prospective clients in a new region required single-order alerts before they would sign. That constrained the first design round to an additive release. Research made clear it would only solve half the problem: users were asking for alerting functionality that already existed, and adding more types to a fragmented system wouldn't fix discoverability. I pushed for the full redesign. When the India team didn't sign and the timeline pressure lifted, that's what shipped.

Original ask

Add single-order alerts with threshold triggers within the existing architecture.

Rescoped to

One unified alert hub: all alert types, all trigger types, consolidated.

Design Decisions

Constraints shaped the first version. Evidence shaped the second.

Ideation

The original scope was shaped by development feasibility before any design work had started. Wireframing early was necessary, not to explore visual direction, but to give PMs and developers something concrete to react to. It reframed the conversation from "what can we build" to "what should we build and is it feasible."

Decision 1

Single-order alerts wouldn't solve the problem

Knowledge transfer sessions established the mismatch early: front office traders monitor too many orders simultaneously for alerting on single-order level to be useful. The development deadline narrowed the options. Multi-order alerting was the only change that could address the actual workflow constraint within the timeline.

Decision 2

Consolidate fragmented alerting and add macro-level triggers

The first prototype made concessions to development feasibility and timeline. The expectation was that the improvements would still move the needle. Validation proved otherwise: users confirmed the added functionality helped, but the core problems (discoverability and limited trigger types) weren't fixed. That feedback made the case for a wider scope: a unified hub consolidating all alert types and macro-level triggers. When the prospective client contract fell through and the timeline pressure lifted, development proceeded with the full redesign.

Reflection

A hunch without evidence doesn't win the argument.

Reflection 1

Instinct without evidence isn't enough to push back

Before design round 1, I had an inkling the scope was too narrow and raised it. Senior colleagues advised me to hold off. Without the research to back it, I didn't have the standing to push further. Once user interviews gave me something concrete, I pushed again and the scope changed. But that came a round later than it could have. Instinct tells you where to look. Evidence is what lets you push.

Reflection 2

Domain research time is design time

Month 1 was entirely knowledge transfer. On a fast-moving project that can feel like lost time. Here it was the opposite: understanding the full alert landscape across the platform was what made it possible to identify fragmentation as a possible root problem, not just missing features.
